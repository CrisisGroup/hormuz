The State of the Strait: Hormuz So Far and What Might Come Next

The blockage of the strait of Hormuz is a contentious element in negotiations to end the U.S.-Israeli war with Iran and an issue of significant interest to regional countries.

by the  INTERNATIONAL CRISIS GROUP

Published XX May, 2026

The blockage of the Strait of Hormuz is a contentious element in negotiations to end the U.S.-Israeli war with Iran and an issue of significant interest to countries nearby and far away.

The strait is an international waterway and the right to transit it is protected by international law. Nonetheless, Tehran’s influence over the strait is a tremendous source of leverage in its negotiations with the U.S. Having spent decades preparing for conflict with a conventionally superior adversary, blocking shipping through the strait was a major though untested component of Tehran’s war planning. After the U.S. and Israel launched their assault on Iran on 28 February, Tehran turned one of world’s most important waterways into a weapon. The U.S. has countered by imposing its own blockade of Iranian ports. Though a ceasefire that began on 7-8 April (depending on time zone) has tamped down the air war that began on 28 February, Iran and the U.S. continue to contest control of the strait.

Iran and the U.S. are both betting that they can inflict sufficient economic pain on their foes to strengthen their position at the negotiating table, while mitigating costs to themselves. Meanwhile, the strait remains closed to most traffic, and the war’s global economic consequences continue to mount.

More Than Energy Moves Through Hormuz

The strait matters because of what passes through it: much of Iran’s and Gulf Arab states’ food imports go west, while a sizable portion of the world’s energy, fertiliser, and other essential commodities transit east. A prolonged disruption of this flow could have vast global economic consequences from spiking transportation costs and fuel shortages to widespread food insecurity as a fertiliser crunch hits more of the world’s staple food planting seasons. Manufacturing of all kinds could also soon suffer from input scarcity.

Among commodities globally traded by sea, flows through the strait of Hormuz account for one-quarter of the crude oil, one-fifth of the liquified natural gas, one-third of the fertiliser, half the sulfur, and one-third of the helium annually. Iran and, to a much lesser extent, the US have stopped the transit of most of these flows.

Crude oil and related products that flow through the strait go mostly to East Asian states, including China, India, Japan, and South Korea. The drop in supply has forced them to undertake stopgap measures, from drawing down reserves to sending an armada of tankers to North American exporters.

In theory, three alternative pipelines across Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Iraq are capable of accommodating approximately half of what typically crossed through Hormuz (albeit at elevated prices), but they too are potentially vulnerable to attack, blocked sea lanes, and domestic politics.

Saudi Arabia’s East-West pipeline can carry up to 7 million barrels per day across the kingdom to the Red Sea port of Yanbu, from where up to 5 million barrels can be made available for export. Its limitations include:

Though the pipeline is buried, the production facilities feeding it, the pumping stations along it, and the terminal facilities at its end could all be targeted; indeed, a pumping station was struck by Iran just after the start of the April ceasefire, briefly knocking out some 700,000 barrels per day of throughput. Riyadh’s main customers are also in east Asia, meaning Red Sea cargoes mostly head south and would therefore fall within range of the Iran-backed Houthis, which could choose to attack commercial shipping should the conflict escalate. Some traffic could also be rerouted north to the Suez canal and the Sumed pipeline, but this would be unlikely to accommodate the full volume of the east-west pipeline.

The Habshan-Fujairah pipeline connects UAE production facilities to its Fujairah port just east of Hormuz, allowing up to 1.5 million barrels per day to bypass the strait. But the port is within range of Iran’s weapons and Iran has attacked it repeatedly during the war - most recently when the US briefly tried to open its own corridor through the strait.

Fertiliser, inputs like ammonia, and natural gas as both input and power source for fertiliser production are major Gulf exports. The global food supply depends on synthetic fertiliser like the urea and phosphate types that pass through the strait.

Because of the restrictions on traffic, the price of the nitrogenous fertiliser urea was up about 40 per cent in global markets in the first half of April. Some of the world’s major grain and oilseed producers like the US, Australia, Brazil, and Bangladesh import large quantities of Gulf fertiliser, for which they will be hard pressed to find substitutes.

Countries where war has already severely disrupted agriculture, such as Sudan - where the UN documented famine in 2024 and 2025 and continues to warn about mass starvation - are even more vulnerable to another price shock.

The growing likelihood of a historically strong El Niño developing later in 2026 further raises the risks to the world’s food production.

Other by-products of oil and natural gas production like sulfur and helium are also critical for the global economy. Sulfur is needed to produce phosphate fertiliser in Morocco, which accounts for up to three-quarters of global proven phosphate reserves, and to process metals like nickel in Indonesia and copper in Chile. Helium is essential in semiconductor manufacturing, with Qatar supplying large quantities to major producers like South Korea and Taiwan.

Sovereignty by Shipping Lane

Ship traffic through the narrow strait normally follows the two-lane Traffic Separation Scheme (TSS) originally proposed by Iran and Oman in 1968 and adopted by the UN’s International Maritime Organisation (IMO).

After the war began and Iran asserted control over the strait, it established an ad hoc route through its own territorial waters and demanded that ships seek Tehran’s approval for passage; use only the new lane; and, for at least some ships - mostly oil tankers - pay a toll potentially running into the low millions of dollars.

This route usually involved looping north around Iran’s Larak island and the levying of charges became known as the ‘Tehran Tollbooth’. Iran-linked ships and certain others sailed this route, including vessels from sponsoring states such as Pakistan, India, or Malaysia, all of which negotiated passage with Tehran.

A new improvised route later emerged as a result of Iran-Oman negotiations, with a small number of ships hugging Oman’s coastline south of the pre-war corridor, seemingly without tolls. By allowing this, Tehran appeared to be sowing division among the members of the Gulf Cooperation Council, to which Oman belongs, most of whom fiercely oppose any scheme to control the strait.

Iran published a map on 9 April of a unilaterally established TSS, a revision of its previous ad hoc route, which set an inbound lane north of Larak island and an outbound lane south of it. According to this new map, both the IMO-approved lanes in the strait’s centre and the recently negotiated Oman corridor were unsafe due to mines whose location Iran claimed it could no longer determine. Transiting ships largely used this route in the following weeks, even including a Qatar-owned LNG tanker heading to Pakistan.

The US began its own blockade of Iran’s southern ports and coast on 13 April and waited about three weeks for Iran to capitulate before attempting to break the standoff by wrenching open the strait rather than. On 3 May, the indefinite ceasefire began breaking down as the US announced plans to ‘guide’ commercial ships through the strait of Hormuz under its new ‘Project Freedom’, beginning the following day. Iran promptly attacked multiple vessels in response.

 The roughly two-day US effort to loosen Iran’s stranglehold on the strait sought to shift the private sector’s perception of risk emanating from Iranian mines and other weapons. It established an ‘air defense umbrella’ covering what it described as a mine-free ‘pathway’ through Oman’s territorial waters - essentially a formalisation of the ad hoc southern corridor previously agreed by Iran and Oman, now in defiance of Tehran. Rather than consistent escorts, the US offered a lane under US air and naval protection for ships to freely – and, supposedly, safely – use. The US did not engage with the shipping industry or its Gulf allies in planning the operation, catching both off guard. It then swiftly abandoned the endeavour, reportedly under pressure from Gulf Arab states who saw it as potentially escalatory.

After the U.S. announced Project Freedom, Iran took a number of steps to regularise its control over the strait through May.

On 4 May, it published yet another map on 4 May, this time showing a claimed ‘maritime control area’ stretching from west of its Qeshm island to the opposite coast and from its own coastline to east of the UAE’s port of Fujairah where its bypass pipeline terminates.

On 5 May, it announced that it had established the ‘Persian Gulf Strait Authority’ to handle coordination of ship transits, including a requirement that they submit details of ownership, insurance, crew, and routing in addition to toll payment arrangements.

On 12 May, Iran then broadened its definition of the strait to include the area between Siri island in the west and Jask port in the east, purporting to enforce its claims by sinking one ship and seizing another off the UAE’s coast on 14 May.

On 18 May, Tehran launched a new insurance scheme in the strait – Hormuz Safe – either as a new, additional fee to ay continuing toll or as a way to legitimise and rebrand the transit fee.

With this shift from an ad hoc corridor to a declared TSS with oversight by a new government body inside an expansive maritime claim, Iran is moving beyond the demand that alternative lanes be used and seeking to formalise a new maritime system - either as a permanent fixture or, at a minimum, as an asset to be bargained away in talks with the U.S.

Battling Blockades and Competing Corridors

The US initiated a blockade of Iran’s southern coast on 13 April. A picket of US warships maintains a blockade line from the Iran-Pakistan border southwest across the Gulf of Oman to Oman’s coast.

Although President Trump initially announced that the US would halt all shipping through the strait of Hormuz, the US military subsequently clarified that the blockade covers ‘vessels of all nations entering or departing Iranian ports and coastal areas’, with exceptions for humanitarian cargos of food, medicine, and other necessities.

By closing off Iran’s exports - especially of crude oil – the U.S. aims to force Iran to relax its own chokehold on the strait and offer other concessions, notably regarding its nuclear programme. Washington hopes that, given limitations on its storage capacity, preventing Iran from exporting its oil will soon compel it to stop producing it, leading to severe, lasting damage to its oilfields.

In practice, the US appeared to begin its blockade selectively. From 13 to 21 April, the energy data firm Vortexa found that 34 Iran-linked tankers transited the US blockade line in either direction - several of which were later intercepted. Subsequent reporting suggested a potential pattern in blockade breaches, namely a greater US focus on oil shipments and greater tolerance for other cargoes such as liquefied petroleum gas. While at least one other firm disputed the claim that the blockade was porous, the US administration itself gave some credence to the reported breaches when it asserted that it judged the blockade’s effectiveness by its effect on Iran’s economy, not by ship movement.

After that first week, the cordon began to solidify. The US reported over 80 ships forcibly rerouted as a result of the blockade, with four would-be blockade runners disabled by US fire, by 18 May.

Only one Iran-linked oil tanker has been confirmed as having made the full journey from loading at Kharg island to east of Malacca since the blockade began.

By mid-May, Iran’s remaining onshore storage was unclear, with potentially just a few weeks of capacity remaining if not full already, consistent with U.S. objectives for the blockade. Once all viable storage options have been exhausted – including empty tankers still running the US blockade – and if export constraints remain, Iran will need to reduce oil output that exceeds its domestic refining capacity. Should production cuts not be sufficiently spread across wells, it will take offline at least some wells. At that point, long-term damage to Iran’s oil production due to field shutdowns - particularly if these occur abruptly and cannot be carefully managed - is possible, especially for older fields.

Yet a range of industry and other analysts have pushed back on the more dire outlooks, presenting these as wishful thinking by the U.S., and noting that Iran has extensive experience calibrating its oil output to match its geopolitical and economic circumstances. It remains unclear whether U.S. pressure or Iranian resilience will prevail.

In any event, Iran may continue to receive payments from oil sales for months to come, irrespective of eventual shutdowns. It already had substantial crude oil and condensate on the water when the blockade began. Estimates vary, but it likely had at least 160 million barrels of crude oil and condensate on tankers either in transit or parked by late April. Although somewhere between 30 and 60 million of those barrels were located within the US blockade area, well over 120 million barrels lie outside of it. If not already purchased or otherwise spoken for, this means that Tehran had the equivalent of three months of its recent daily exports afloat beyond the US blockade when it started.  Even absent floating storage, Iran potentially could mortgage future oil sales to China, earning revenue on oil not yet extracted - a previously common arrangement China employed in its Belt and Road Initiative - if it has not done so already. Of course, for Iran to receive revenue from the sale of floating oil assumes that it can continue to circumvent ever-tightening sanctions. For now, China appears to have shrugged off intensifying US sanctions on the Iran oil trade.

Should it choose to do so, the US could escalate its crackdown on Iran oil sales by going after Iran’s floating storage. Already, it has seized at least three Iran-linked ships, including two oil tankers in the Indian ocean. Iran responded in kind on 22 April, using swarms of fast attack boats to board and seize two ships attempting to pass through the strait - its first capture of ships in the war, facilitated by the ceasefire and the lack of hostile US aircraft overhead.

The US could ramp up its disabling attacks or seizures in three areas where Iranian tankers were concentrated as of 1 May: within the US blockade area (mainly the Gulf of Oman); in the sea lane linking the Gulf of Oman to the strait of Malacca; and in a well-known hotspot for illicit ship-to-ship transfers and floating storage east of Malaysia. Other early May hotspots outside US reach include areas near Hong Kong and off the Chinese coast in the Yellow sea.

US oil tanker seizures to date used the middle option - the route to Malacca, one of the world’s busiest sea lanes. So far, the US has not targeted the usual gathering area for Iran-linked tankers off Malaysia, possibly out of concern that Iran would counter-escalate with strikes in the region, and because the closer US seizures get to China, the more geopolitically sensitive they become. Seizures inside the US blockade would be at risk of attack by Iran and could trigger further escalation. That said, the US ultimately may decide the risks are worth it if it exhausts other options to no avail.

Some analysts have speculated that the US could also cut off essential Iranian imports by directly blocking ships or scaring off companies from attempting to reach Iran despite the blockade’s theoretical carveouts. Under this theory, intentionally cutting off imports of goods as crucial as grain – which typically come through Hormuz – would compel Iran to surrender at the negotiating table even more quickly than stopping its hydrocarbon output.

But the unlawful weaponization of starvation, which this scenario appears to contemplate, likewise rests on a questionable premise given Iran’s domestic grain production and its alternative import routes for bulk grain. While Iran imports considerable volumes of grains and oilseeds annually, much of it is in the form of livestock feed, the total loss of which would likely damage its livestock industry and economy severely but not leave its cities bereft of bread. The Iranian wheat harvest begins in June, accounting for most of the wheat consumed in the country, and growing conditions have been broadly favourable. The balance would typically come through the strait to Iran’s main grain-handling port Imam Khomeini, or smaller alternatives like Chabahar.

Iran also has recourse to lifelines like the International North-South Transportation Corridor (INSTC), covering maritime routes through the Caspian Sea and rail corridors to either of its sides, in addition to further rail connections to China and road connections with neighbours like Pakistan and its major ports. Caspian and rail export capacity have ramped up in recent years, spurred on by Russia’s efforts since 2022 to circumvent US and European sanctions in response to its invasion of Ukraine.

The INSTC faces its own logistical challenges despite recent investment, but still offers alternative routes for grain and oilseed from major producers such as Russia and Kazakhstan. In recent years, both Russia and Kazakhstan have delivered large volumes of grain to Iran as Caspian and rail volumes have grown.

Even under a scenario where the U.S. attempts to blockade food, there probably will not be an absolute scarcity of staples like wheat, but that is no guarantee of food remaining affordable for the majority of Iranians, especially urban dwellers. In this respect, the blockade could still threaten to provoke a food price crisis - and possibly cripple the Iranian livestock sector – even if does not soon threaten mass starvation.

Yet the blockade will still hurt Iran enormously. Iranian sources linked to its security services publicly reported that only 40 per cent of the country’s trade could be rerouted away from blockaded ports. Alternative corridors may keep vital imports coming in, but that may not be enough to stave off economic crisis and civil unrest over time. Indeed, the crisis has already begun: inflation soared to 67 per cent in April as millions lost jobs.

Whether the US and Iran will now negotiate or escalate is unclear. The US blockade has been more porous than many imagined, while Iran’s resilience seem to have surprised some cheerleaders for Washington and Tel Aviv’s war. Both still have many escalatory options. In an unmistakable message, Iran published a map of undersea cables digitally connecting the region to the rest of the world, along with related infrastructure, and warned about their potential vulnerability and threatened to tax them. The prospect of economic collapse could yet force it to increase attacks in the region – whether on US forces or, more likely, its Gulf Arab neighbours and their economies.

The US may yet tighten its blockade, or make good on some of its threats to resume hostilities and destroy Iran’s critical infrastructure, possibly again with Israel’s collaboration. Such escalation could borrow a page from their anti-Houthi playbook, when the US attempted to erase the main port for importing fuel into Houthi territory while Israel incrementally bombed the two ports handling grain and other essential imports. In any escalation scenario, the economic consequences are ruinous.

A Different Proliferation Problem

The Hormuz standoff will eventually end, probably as part of a wider agreement to end the war. Any arrangement regarding the strait agreed to or even just tolerated by the US and Iran would have implications for freedom of navigation globally. This would certainly be the case if the arrangement included charging ships for passing through this international waterway – a clear contravention of widely accepted international law governing straits like Hormuz, in addition to US sanctions. (In contrast, fees can be charged for transit through man-made canals).

For Iran, control of the strait has become a considerable strategic asset. It views it as a way to exert influence and extract revenue by adjusting the toll price and currency in which it is paid, changing the number of allowable crossings per day, or barring passage for certain states, nationals and types of cargo altogether. This ability to unilaterally influence global commodity prices would provide the Islamic Republic with powerful leverage in any negotiations, a future deterrent against foreign pressure or military action, as well as a potential source of windfall revenues.

Tehran may also view collecting tolls as its only reliable way to fund post-war reconstruction, which will be desperately needed as a result of the war, especially in light of Israel’s attacks on hard-currency generating industries such as petrochemicals and steel, as well as the wider damage caused by U.S.-Israeli bombardments. This need could be mitigated by the US unfreezing an estimated $100 billion in Iranian funds held abroad, or sanctions relief, the value of which – depending on its scope – could dwarf potential oil tanker toll revenues.

Even if US arguments about freedom of navigation are hypocritical after its own recent battering of the principle - Washington is currently engaged in a de facto fuel blockade of Cuba after a similar effort against Venezuela, and the US has seized Russian-linked ships using at times legally questionable justifications - Iran’s assertion of sovereignty over Hormuz, and a right to toll shipping there, risks a proliferation of expansive maritime sovereignty claims and chokepoint tolls.

Among other possible implications of Iranian control over the strait is increased risk of a similar, previously rumoured Houthi Red Sea toll - a possible replacement for reduced Iranian support - and of coastal states astride other key crossings like the strait of Malacca following suit, especially when they incur costs in keeping those waterways free of pirates and other threats.

The reactions of the three states abutting the strait of Malacca illustrate this concern. Indonesia’s finance minister recently mused about a Malacca toll, although he quickly walked the statement back. A toll comparable to what Iran has publicly suggested would mean annual Malacca transit fees of around $8.5 billion. For its part, Malaysia has maintained an open channel with Tehran and is one of the few states to have negotiated the transit of ships through Hormuz, with Iran describing this arrangement a sign of Tehran was not forgetting its friends. Singapore, meanwhile, has condemned the idea of negotiating with Iran on Hormuz transits and rejected the idea of the first Malacca toll in hundreds of years. This may in part reflect Singapore’s role as a major refinery and ship fuelling hub - its economy is better served by maximising Malacca transits rather than prompting rerouting through nearby straits of which it is not a part.

One analogue to Iran’s actions might be the Sound Dues levied by Denmark in the Danish straits until 1857, which charged 1-5 per cent of cargo value depending on several factors such as nationality. Some have offered the Montreux Convention - a multilateral treaty allowing Turkiye to charge cost-recovery for services rendered to Turkish straits transits - as a way out of the impasse over Hormuz. Ankara reported fees worth just under $230 million from over 51,000 transits in 2024 - hardly a lucrative toll and much less than what Iran has proposed. Cost-recovery may be an option for Hormuz, but likely one that only works if Iran wants its right to levy a fee in Hormuz recognised more than it needs the much higher revenue from a true toll.

If Iran’s proposed levy was to spread to other waterways, many voyages would be charged multiple times. Oil from the Persian Gulf would incur payments at Hormuz and again at Malacca on the way to major east Asia customers - doubling the cost of a flat fee toll model. Other ways of taxing shipping may spread, too. One can envision a scenario in which oil from the Baltic heading to India might pay a percentage of cargo value at the Danish straits (punishing when oil prices rise), cost-recovery fees at the strait of Gibraltar (adding friction but not meaningful cost), and a flat fee at the Bab al Mandeb (biting more when oil prices fall), on top of existing and legal Suez canal fees.
